Six gentle poses to start with

Read each one slowly. Every pose has a seated version, and you should only ever move within a range that feels comfortable. When in doubt, do less.

Before you begin: warm up with a minute of easy shoulder rolls and slow breathing, keep a sturdy chair within reach, and stop if anything causes sharp pain, dizziness or breathlessness. New to exercise or managing a health condition? Please read our health disclaimer and talk to your doctor first.

Seated

1. Seated Mountain

The foundation of every chair practice — it teaches you to sit tall, breathe fully and switch on the muscles that support good posture.

How to do it

  1. Sit toward the front of a sturdy chair, feet flat and hip-width apart.
  2. Lengthen your spine as if a thread lifts the crown of your head.
  3. Breathe in and float both arms out and up; relax your shoulders down.
  4. Hold for three slow breaths, then lower the arms on an exhale.

Good for: posture, breathing, shoulder mobility.

Seated

2. Gentle Seated Twist

A soothing rotation that keeps the spine mobile and can ease lower-back tension and sluggish digestion.

How to do it

  1. Sit tall, feet flat. Rest your right hand on your left knee.
  2. On an exhale, turn gently from the waist to the left, letting your gaze follow.
  3. Keep the twist soft — your shoulders, not your neck, lead the movement.
  4. Hold two breaths, return to centre, then repeat to the other side.

Good for: spinal mobility, back comfort, digestion.

Easy

3. Seated Cat–Cow

A slow, breath-led wave through the spine. One of the kindest ways to loosen a stiff back first thing in the morning.

How to do it

  1. Sit tall with hands resting on your thighs or knees.
  2. Breathe in, gently arch — chest forward, shoulders back (Cow).
  3. Breathe out, round the back — chin toward chest, navel drawing in (Cat).
  4. Flow slowly for five to eight breaths, led entirely by your breath.

Good for: back stiffness, posture, gentle warm-up.

Balance

4. Supported Tree

A confidence-building balance pose. With one hand on a chair, you train the steadying muscles that help prevent falls.

How to do it

  1. Stand tall behind or beside a sturdy chair, one hand resting on it.
  2. Shift your weight onto one foot. Rest the other toe lightly on the floor, or against the ankle.
  3. Stand tall and breathe steadily for a few breaths.
  4. Lower and switch sides. Only lift the foot higher when you feel ready.

Good for: balance, ankle & hip strength, fall prevention.

Balance

5. Gentle Warrior

A grounded standing pose that builds leg strength and a feeling of steadiness and confidence.

How to do it

  1. Stand with feet comfortably wide, a chair within reach.
  2. Turn your right foot out and softly bend that knee over the ankle.
  3. Let your arms rise to a comfortable height; keep your shoulders relaxed.
  4. Hold two or three breaths, straighten, and switch sides.

Good for: leg strength, stability, stamina.

Easy

6. Rest & Relax

Never skip the rest. A few minutes of supported stillness lets your nervous system settle and the benefits of practice sink in.

How to do it

  1. Sit back comfortably in your chair, or lie down with knees supported by a cushion.
  2. Let your hands rest, palms up, and soften your face and jaw.
  3. Breathe slowly and naturally for two to five minutes.
  4. To finish, wiggle fingers and toes, then rise slowly.

Good for: relaxation, stress relief, better sleep.
