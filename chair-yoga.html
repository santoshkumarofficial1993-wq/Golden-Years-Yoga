The complete chair yoga guide for seniors

Everything you need to begin safely from a chair — what it is, who it helps, how to stay safe, and a simple ten-minute routine you can follow today.

Chair yoga takes the gentlest, most beneficial parts of yoga — mindful movement, breathing and balance — and brings them to the support of a sturdy chair. It removes the two things that stop most older adults from starting: getting down to and up from the floor, and the fear of losing balance. What's left is pure benefit, accessible to almost everyone.

What is chair yoga?

Chair yoga is a style of yoga practised either seated on a chair or standing while using a chair for support. Poses are adapted so you can enjoy the same stretches, strengthening and breathing as a traditional class, without kneeling, lying on the floor, or balancing unaided. It is one of the most studied and recommended forms of movement for older adults precisely because it is so low-risk.

In one sentence: chair yoga is gentle, supported movement that helps you stay flexible, strong, steady and calm — at any age and almost any ability.

The benefits for older adults

Done little and often, chair yoga can help you:

  • Move more comfortably — gentle range-of-motion work eases stiff joints and the ache of arthritis.
  • Stay steady — balance and lower-body strengthening reduce the risk of falls.
  • Breathe and relax — slow, paired breathing calms the nervous system and supports better sleep.
  • Keep your independence — the strength to rise from a chair, reach a high shelf and walk with confidence.
  • Lift your mood — mindful movement is a well-recognised support for everyday wellbeing.

Who it's perfect for

Chair yoga is especially well-suited to anyone who is new to exercise, recovering from illness or surgery, managing arthritis or joint replacements, living with reduced stamina or balance, or simply more comfortable not getting down to the floor. If you can sit in a chair and breathe, you can begin.

Staying safe

Gentle does not mean careless. A few simple habits keep your practice safe and enjoyable:

  • Use a firm, armless, non-wheeled chair on a non-slip surface.
  • Sit toward the front of the seat with both feet flat on the floor.
  • Move slowly and within a comfortable range — never force or bounce.
  • Keep breathing naturally; never hold your breath through effort.
  • Stop immediately for sharp pain, chest pain, dizziness or breathlessness, and seek advice.
  • Keep water nearby and wear non-slip footwear or practise barefoot.

Please check first. This guide is educational and not medical advice. If you have a health condition, take medication that affects balance or blood pressure, or are recovering from surgery, speak with your doctor or physiotherapist before starting. See our full health disclaimer.

A simple 10-minute routine

Here is a complete daily sequence. Move at your own pace and rest whenever you need to.

  1. Settle & breathe (1 min). Sit tall, feet flat, and take five slow breaths.
  2. Neck & shoulders (2 min). Slow shoulder rolls, then gently look left and right.
  3. Seated Mountain (1 min). Reach the arms up on an inhale; repeat three times.
  4. Seated Cat–Cow (2 min). Flow with the breath to mobilise the spine.
  5. Gentle Twist (2 min). One soft twist to each side, two breaths each.
  6. Rest & Relax (2 min). Sit back, soften, and breathe slowly to finish.

That's it. Repeat most days and add a standing balance pose like Supported Tree when you feel ready.

Common questions

How often should I practise?

Aim for a short session most days. Ten minutes daily brings more benefit than one long weekly session.

Will it actually make me stronger?

Yes. Supported movement steadily builds the muscles you use for standing, walking and balancing — the ones that protect your independence.

What should I wear?

Comfortable, non-restrictive clothing and either bare feet or non-slip shoes. No special gear required.
